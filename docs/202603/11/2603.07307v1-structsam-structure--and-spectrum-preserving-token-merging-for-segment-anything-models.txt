## Abstract 

Recent token merging techniques for Vision Transformers (ViTs) provide substantial speedups by reducing the number of tokens processed by self-attention, often without retraining. However, their direct application to the Segment Anything Model (SAM) family is nontrivial: SAM’s image encoder mixes windowed and global attention, and its mask decoder relies on dense, prompt-conditioned features for precise boundary pre-diction. We systematically evaluate representa-tive token-merging methods on SAM and Medi-cal SAM in a strict off-the-shelf setting, and find that existing destination-selection heuristics can erode boundaries and leak prompt information as merge rates increase. We propose Struct-SAM , a resolution-preserving merge–unmerge framework tailored to SAM. StructSAM com-putes a lightweight token-energy score from first-order feature gradients, uses grid-based flatness screening to protect boundary/prompt regions, and merges tokens within flat areas toward low-energy destinations with explicit token recovery. We further provide a spectral graph coarsening view showing that score-guided merging yields bounded Laplacian spectral distortion compared to random or window-restricted baselines. ## 1. Introduction 

SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) has recently emerged as a foun-dation model for segmentation, demonstrating remarkable generalization across diverse visual domains. By combining a powerful Vision Transformer (ViT) image encoder (Doso-vitskiy, 2020) with prompt-conditioned mask decoding, SAM enables flexible, interactive segmentation using points, boxes, and masks, often without any task-specific training. This versatility has led to rapid adoption beyond natural im-ages, including medical imaging, e.g., MedSAM (Ma et al., 2024), robot surgery (Wang et al., 2023a), and embodied AI systems (Li et al., 2025; Noh et al., 2025), where robust seg-mentation is a critical component. Despite these strengths, SAM’s practical deployment is severely constrained by its computational cost (Zhang et al., 2023). In particular, SAM follows a heavy encoder–light decoder design: for large variants such as ViT-L and ViT-H, the image encoder alone accounts for over 98% of the total model parameters and FLOPs, making inference expensive even when the down-stream task is lightweight or interactive. Recognizing this bottleneck, recent efforts to improve SAM’s efficiency have largely focused on model com-pression strategies, including knowledge distillation to smaller architectures (Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2025), lightweight re-designs of the vision backbone (Xiong et al., 2024), and post-training quantization (Lv et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). While effective, these approaches typically require retraining or fine-tuning the image encoder on large-scale datasets or applying task-specific calibration during deployment. Such requirements limit their applicability in scenarios where SAM is used off-the-shelf, where training data is unavailable, or where domain-specific fine-tuning is 1

undesirable due to cost and stability concerns. In practice, SAM’s pre-trained representations are already highly expressive across many domains, motivating ap-proaches that retain the original model weights while re-ducing inference cost . Token merging (Bolya et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2024) has recently emerged as a promising strat-egy for accelerating Vision Transformers by dynamically reducing the number of tokens processed by self-attention layers. By grouping similar patch tokens, token merging has shown substantial gains in both classification (Bolya et al., 2022) and segmentation tasks (Norouzi et al., 2024) for standard ViTs, often without retraining. However, di-rectly applying existing token merging methods to SAM is nontrivial . Unlike conventional ViTs, (i) SAM’s image encoder interleaves windowed and global attention and pre-serves fine-grained spatial details, which is crucial for mask prediction. Moreover, (ii) segmentation tasks inherently 

require dense, structured outputs , making aggressive token reduction (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023; Kim et al., 2024) incom-patible without careful unmerging or feature upsampling mechanisms. Motivated by these challenges, we systematically study rep-resentative token-merging techniques originally developed for ViTs in images and videos (Bolya et al., 2023; Tran et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) and dense segmentation set-tings (Norouzi et al., 2024; Bolya & Hoffman, 2023), and adapt them to the SAM family, including SAM and Medi-cal SAM. We evaluate commonly used SAM backbones (ViT-B and ViT-L) on boundary-sensitive natural-image benchmarks and on cross-domain medical segmentation datasets. Crucially, all experiments are conducted in a strict off-the-shelf setting , without any fine-tuning, to assess the accuracy–efficiency limits of inference-time token merging for foundation segmentation models. Our findings show that prior approaches, which typically rely on random, global, or window-restricted destination selection, struggle to preserve object boundaries and prompt-relevant regions, leading to noticeable degradation as the merge rate increases. To mitigate this issue, we propose a structure- and spectrum-preserving token-merging framework tailored to SAM-style architectures, called StructSAM. StructSAM (i) identifies boundary-critical tokens using a lightweight energy score computed from first-order finite differences on the encoder feature map (Ziou & Tabbone, 1998; Forsyth & Ponce, 2002), inspired by connections to spectral graph energy (Bal-akrishnan, 2004; Gutman & Zhou, 2006). Tokens near object boundaries exhibit large feature gradients and are therefore protected from merging, while tokens in visually flat regions can be safely merged. Building on this distinc-tion, StructSAM (ii) groups tokens into grid-based cells and ranks cells by flatness to select mergeable regions with spatial coherence, and (iii) merges tokens within selected cells toward low-energy destinations while explicitly un-merging to recover the original token resolution required by SAM’s mask decoder. When box prompts are available, a prompt-aware variant further restricts aggressive merging to tokens outside the prompted region for additional speedups. Finally, we show that our merging strategy admits a spectral graph-theoretic interpretation (Jin et al., 2020; Tran et al., 2024), under which it provably preserves intrinsic spectral properties of the original token space under mild condi-tions, offering a principled explanation for its stability and effectiveness. In summary, our work contributes the following points: • We present the first systematic evaluation of inference-time token merging for the SAM family (SAM and MedSAM) in a strict off-the-shelf setting, reveal-ing why existing merging strategies can fail under boundary- and prompt-sensitive segmentation. • We propose StructSAM, a boundary- and prompt-aware, structure-preserving merging strategy that lever-ages gradient-based token energy and cell flatness to protect informative regions while merging redun-dant background tokens. StructSAM achieves strong accuracy–efficiency trade-offs, reducing FLOPs by 25– 30% (and up to 40%+ with prompt-aware merging) while maintaining segmentation quality across natural and medical benchmarks. • We provide a spectral graph-theoretic analysis show-ing that, under mild assumptions, our score-guided merging yields a provable bound on spectral distortion, offering a principled explanation for its robustness in dense segmentation and the limitations of prior random or similarity-only merging approaches. 

## 2. Related Work 

Compression and Pruning Methods for SAM. To im-prove SAM’s efficiency, most existing methods focus on 

backbone replacement or structured compression . Ap-proaches such as MobileSAM (Zhang et al., 2023), Fast-SAM (Zhao et al., 2023), EdgeSAM (Zhou et al., 2025), and EfficientSAM (Xiong et al., 2024) replace the origi-nal ViT-H encoder with lightweight architectures, including TinyViT (Wu et al., 2022), or EfficientViT (Zhang et al., 2024), often combined with task-specific training strategies. While effective, these methods typically require training new models from scratch, incurring high data and training costs and potentially weakening SAM’s pre-trained gener-alization. More recent work compresses the original SAM via structured pruning and distillation (Chen et al., 2024), which better preserves pre-trained knowledge but introduces additional optimization complexity and limited flexibility across domains and hardware settings. 2Structure- and Spectrum-Preserving Token Merging for Segment Anything Models 

In contrast, StructSAM investigates SAM off-the-shelf ac-celeration via inference-time token merging, without mod-ifying model weights or requiring retraining, offering a lightweight alternative to architectural compression. 

Token Pruning and Merging in Transformers. Exist-ing methods for reducing transformer complexity mainly fall into dynamic token pruning and token merging. Token pruning has been explored in both NLP (Goyal et al., 2020; Zhong et al., 2023) and vision transformers (Yin et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b), where tokens are dynamically removed based on learned importance. These methods typically re-quire training and introduce input-dependent token counts, complicating batching and limiting practical speedups. To-ken merging methods, led by ToMe (Bolya et al., 2023) and follow-up works (Chen et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024), merge similar tokens using lightweight bipartite matching, achiev-ing better efficiency-accuracy trade-offs. While attention-or guidance-based improvements exist, these approaches remain sensitive to token partitioning and distribution imbal-ance. More principled clustering or graph-based methods (Loukas & Vandergheynst, 2018; Tran et al., 2024) offer stronger guarantees but incur substantial overhead, making them less suitable for efficient ViT inference. However, these methods typically reduce tokens progressively across layers , which is not well-suited to SAM’s architecture and dense segmentation setting. 

Token Reduction for Semantic Segmentation. A num-ber of methods have been developed to reduce tokens in transformer-based segmentation. Early token halting ap-proaches pause high-confidence tokens to avoid further pro-cessing, but this can limit information flow in later lay-ers (Tang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). Token clustering strategies such as ELViT (Liang et al., 2022) and Ailu-Rus (Li et al., 2023) merge neighboring tokens within a single layer, yet offer limited efficiency gains due to cluster-ing overhead and one-shot reduction. Content-aware token sharing (Lu et al., 2023) uses a policy network to merge im-age patches, but it incurs additional computational cost and primarily addresses local redundancies. More recent work, ALGM (Norouzi et al., 2024), performs token merging in two adaptive stages: first locally in early layers and then globally in intermediate layers, based on cosine similarity, achieving substantial throughput improvements and even enhancing mIoU without retraining. In comparison, our algorithm introduces a structure-aware token-merging strategy that uses image-gradient-based en-ergy scores to identify protected boundary tokens and merge-able regions, thereby avoiding the random or purely local window–based merging decisions commonly adopted in existing approaches.

## 4. Graph Coarsening View and Spectral Stability in StructSAM

## 5. Experiments 

5.1. Experimental Setup Datasets. We evaluate our method on a diverse set of benchmarks that emphasize boundary precision, struc-tural detail preservation, and cross-domain robustness un-der token reduction. Boundary-focused datasets include DIS5K (Qin et al., 2022), which contains high-resolution images with pixel-accurate annotations of complex object boundaries; T HIN OBJECT -5K and COIFT (Liew et al., 2021), which target globally thin structures (e.g., wires and poles) and objects with fine interior thin structures, respec-tively; and HRSOD (Zeng et al., 2019), which requires precise boundary delineation in high-resolution scenes. To assess generalization, we additionally evaluate on COCO (Lin et al., 2014) for diverse natural images, CITYSCAPES (Cordts et al., 2016) for high-resolution urban driving scenes, and ADE20K (Zhou et al., 2019) for holistic scene parsing across 150 semantic categories. Finally, we include IN BREAST (Moreira et al., 2012), a full-field digital mammography repository containing 410 high-resolution DICOM mammograms with polygonal segmentation anno-tations, where MedSAM (Ma et al., 2024) demonstrates SAM’s adaptability. 

Metrics We evaluate our method in terms of both seg-mentation quality and efficiency . Quality is measured using 

mIoU and Boundary mIoU , the latter focusing on accu-racy near object boundaries and being particularly sensitive to token merging. Efficiency is assessed via FLOPs and 

peak GPU memory usage . Together, these metrics capture the quality–efficiency trade-off of different token merging strategies. 

Baseline. We compare our method against five representa-tive token merging baselines. T OME (Bolya et al., 2023) in-troduced bipartite soft matching to efficiently reduce tokens in ViTs. ToMeSD (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023) adapted this approach for diffusion models with timestep-aware merg-ing. PiToMe (Tran et al., 2024) improved merging qual-ity through pivot-based selection that preserves important tokens. VidToMe (Li et al., 2024) extended token merg-ing to exploit temporal redundancy in video transformers. ALGM (Norouzi et al., 2024) proposed adaptive local-global balancing for dense prediction tasks. While these methods achieve substantial efficiency gains, they do not explicitly consider gradient information to identify task-critical tokens. 5.2. Results 

We evaluate segmentation quality under different token merge rates on high-quality datasets, including DIS5K-VD, HRSOD, and ThinObject5K-TE, as shown in Fig. 6 and Tab. 3. PiToMe and ToMe incur O(N 2) computational complexity in their token-merging algorithms, which limits the maximum merge rate to 50%. In contrast, other methods indiscriminately treat all tokens as merge candidates and sample them as the destination set , leading to significant performance degradation on these highly detailed datasets. Tab. 1 evaluates token compression methods on the INbreast mammography dataset using MedSAM, measuring com-putational cost (GFLOPs) and segmentation quality (Dice Score). Our method achieves 347.8 GFLOPs with a Dice score of 74.81, incurring only a 0.62-point drop from the baseline (75.43) while reducing computation by 28.5%. In contrast, competing methods exhibit substantially larger quality degradations despite comparable or higher compu-tational costs: ToMeSD (-2.10 Dice at 362.3 GFLOPs), VidToMe (-2.11 Dice at 399.8 GFLOPs), and ALGM (-5.60 Dice at 381.1 GFLOPs). Notably, our prompt-aware variant further reduces computation to 283.05 GFLOPs, a 41.8% reduction from baseline, while maintaining a Dice score of 74.72, demonstrating that leveraging bounding box prompts enables more aggressive token reduction in background re-gions without compromising segmentation accuracy. These results on medical imaging data indicate that our approach generalizes effectively beyond natural images, achieving superior efficiency-accuracy trade-offs in the clinically rele-vant domain of breast lesion segmentation.

## 6. Conclusion 

We present the first systematic study of token merging for SAM-based segmentation, showing that methods designed for classification ViTs often fail to preserve structure in dense, prompt-conditioned settings. Motivated by this gap, we introduce StructSAM, based on the insight that first-order, gradient-based energy cues are sufficient to identify boundary-critical tokens without costly graph interactions. By framing token merging as a local, structure-driven pro-cess with explicit recovery, we provide an efficient and prin-cipled approach to scaling foundation segmentation models. 

## Impact Statement 

This work enables efficient deployment of foundation seg-mentation models in low-resource environments by substan-tially reducing inference cost without retraining or archi-tectural modification. By preserving fine-grained structural details under aggressive token reduction, our method broad-ens access to high-quality segmentation for applications with limited compute, memory, or energy budgets, includ-ing medical imaging and embedded vision systems.

## A. Limitations 

Despite these advantages, StructSAM has several limitations. First, our current formulation relies on fixed, hand-crafted gradient operators (e.g., Sobel or central differences), which may be suboptimal for highly textured regions or domain-specific imagery. Second, the use of predefined cell partitions introduces an additional design choice that may require tuning for different input resolutions or architectures. Finally, while our method is evaluated primarily in the context of SAM-based segmentation, its effectiveness for other vision tasks or transformer architectures remains to be fully explored. These limitations suggest several promising directions for future work. An interesting extension would be to learn adaptive or task-specific gradient operators that retain the efficiency of first-order information while improving robustness. In addition, dynamically adjusting cell structures or sampling strategies based on content or model depth could further enhance flexibility. More broadly, we believe that StructSAM opens up new opportunities for rethinking token merging as a local, structure-driven problem rather than a global graph optimization task, and we hope this perspective will inspire more efficient designs for scalable vision transformers as well as other relevant domain applications in 3D perception learning (Tran et al., 2025) or robotic manipulation with object-centric conditional inputs (Chung et al., 2026; Hanyu et al., 2026; Vo et al., 2025).
